Jeepney Blues

Previously published in Science Fiction: Filipino Fiction for Young Adults

by Kim Sarabia




We fought all the time. People said that's just what brothers do. Except that there was nothing normal about the way Monkey and I fought. He knew the exact places to cause the worst kind of pain. I knew his old injuries. I kicked the crook behind his right knee, and he snarled. It was an old wound that never quite healed from when a water rat snuck a piece of broken glass into the floating rings. I knew it was playing dirty, hitting that old spot, but my brother was twice my size. I was small and could barely lift the vat I used to cook lugaw. When you're a weakling born into a hellhole like this, there’s no shame in using a dirty trick.

“Let’s go, Toto,” Monkey said, limping towards me, his arms suddenly held up in surrender. My eyes darted towards the fake spacer ID tags in his fist. The ones he’d just earned from his last fight in the floating rings.

“I told you for the thousandth time, gago,” I said, coughing. I felt like puking from an uppercut he threw into my side. “I never wanted to go.”

     “Oh I'm the idiot?” he asked, scratching his head in annoyance.  There was a gash on his left brow, from where I managed to smack him with my skillet. "I'm telling you that I can get us out of here and you try to attack me?”

I ignored him and crawled back to the bench inside the jeepney. It bobbed up and down under my weight. Monkey stayed outside, standing on top of the rubble we had docked next to. Behind him were the remains of what probably used to be a shopping mall. Only the topmost floor peeked through the water. Clothes, toys, and other junk floated up between the decay and rubble that filled these waters.

     “You must have been hit in the head one too many times,” I said.  With that, he went still.  Making fun of his brainpower always caught his attention.  “You’d have to drag me into the bowels of whatever damned shuttle you plan to hitch a ride on.”

     He barked out a deep laugh.  “That won’t be hard.”

My blood rushed to my head, and before my brain could even catch up with the rest of my body, I found myself jumping out of the jeepney again and lunging at him.

"You can't make me do squat!"

My fists ended nowhere, and he dodged me easily even with his bad leg. I hated him even more, and my fury made me see white. Monkey barely talked, yet his cocky expression never let me forget that he was the one in charge. He was the elder. Mama’s words boomed in my mind: Listen to your kuya

"You're right," he said. “And why should I? We’re done here.”

With that, he shoved me into the filthy waters. I've fallen in many times, but it did not get better with each incident. I could sense dead things against my skin. The steaming, rotting stench surged up my nostrils and made me gag. The waters were so thick that I was being pushed to the surface, yet I could feel death and decay just underneath my toes, wanting to pull me under.

I reached out and grabbed the back of his ankle as he turned to leave.

"You think you'd get very far up there in the space colonies doing the shit jobs?" I laughed. "They'll never let you get on those starships."

I saw the tic in his jaw, and I winced, readying for another blow. None came. I thought I could predict his next move after all these years watching him in the floating rings, but I guess it’d been a while since I’ve been to his fights. He was twelve when he first started. Back then, he was just a human punching bag. He was forced to face men twice his age. I vomited in terror after witnessing his first few fights, and he would have to pat my back as I dry-heaved over the water, while his face was still bloody. I would have died a long time ago if it wasn't for Monkey improving year after year, until he became the best, and all bets were placed on him. In the past couple of years, we never had to worry about starving. Yet he never held any of this over my head.

There’s no reason I should ever cross him after all he’s done, but all this just made me shake and want to fling more things at him.

“Anything will be better than here,” he said, pointing to the jeepney that was already too small for both of us. It’s been our home all our lives, and we were once small enough to play tag inside. Our mother found it amidst mountains of scrap heap and fixed it up to float. It was a gaudy chrome vehicle with trinkets on the side and hood that used to bus people from one place to another when this part of the watery wastes was Metro Manila.

“We belong here,” I said. “We were born here.”

He never said much. When he was not fighting, Monkey spent most of his time stargazing, trying to find the twinkling light of the space stations and Mars. That's where all the people had gone after the islands were swallowed up by the sea hundreds of years ago.

     “Last chance, Toto” he frowned, staring at the sludge dripping down my face, as I climbed back onto a flat surface that used to be an awning.

"Go to hell."

He looked down, his mouth pulled tight. He took the glider from the roof of the jeepney, and without another word, sped off.


#

          Still dizzy, I went back into the jeepney. It did not take much for it to look like a disaster. I panicked after Monkey charged at me during the fight, and grabbed for anything within hands reach. The whole interior was coated with all of my spices.  I hung up the pots that were turned over, and held my breath as I tested the burner. I sighed with relief when the flame appeared.  Mama was the one who built the jeepney to float.  She knew how everything worked, and would have been the only one who could fix it.

     On the side opposite of the kitchen, all of my things were scattered throughout the floor.  My mildewy comic books, black vinyl records, and dozens of discolored action figures were everywhere. My favorite, a man that looked like a lion, lay with his plastic arm outstretched in a puddle of soup. Monkey found it a long time ago and gave it to me on my seventh birthday.  I propped the lion man back up on a stack of books.  I held up what was left of the hammock that Monkey and I took turns sleeping on.  There was a tear that stretched to either side from when his massive body went through the fabric during the fight.

After cleaning the soup off the floor, I made sure to heat up a new vat of lugaw. I added a sack of rice and clean tank water into the drum, topped it off with some salt, and put the burner on low. In an hour, the air would be filled with the smell of warm rice porridge. My customers got irritable when there wasn’t a batch ready to be served, and I knew they’d be coming soon.

I swung onto the roof from the driver’s side.  I washed up using water from our tank reserves.  Crouching down, I faced the coop that held our last remaining chicken.  It clucked at me.

“You gotta back me up next time,” I said. “We only have each other now.”

The jeepney swayed gently under my feet.  I looked out at the expanse.  A brown sea overflowing with garbage, half-sunken buildings, and decay surrounded me.  For sixteen years, I’ve stared at the same stagnant waters.  It was easy to identify the murky trail where he had gone off on his glider.  His wake cut through the muck, snaking around the filthy waters, towards the horizon.

     Pain curled up inside my chest.  Not because I regretted throwing those insults at him.  Definitely not because I just took the worst beating in living memory. It’s because Monkey’s departure felt normal and expected. Family came and went. Even Mama ran away. In the watery wastes, no one stayed too long in your life, and Monkey was never supposed to stick around, making sure a weakling like me survived.

#

“Goddamn logging, goddamn mining, goddamn factories!”

Tom was in one of his fits, just as he and other customers drifted in towards the jeepney.

His voice always quivered, mostly spouting nonsense that no one ever understood.

“Landslides, earthquakes, storms, all because of them,” he whined, pointing to the sky, spittle dripping from the sides of his lips. “Now they live in a floating castle. And we get to eat their scraps!”

“Scraps or not, you still gotta eat with your mouth closed,” I warned him.

He loved to eat his lugaw with fried garlic on top.  Every time I sprinkled some, he would call me stingy then bang his bowl against the counter until I added more.

Flor took a seat next to Tom.  She cruised in a tiny pirogue that she rigged with a fast motor.  Flor survived out in the watery wastes by entertaining different men, although she hosted the occasional spacer.  She preferred to have the works on her lugaw: a hardboiled egg, chicken and ground peanuts all doused in soy sauce.  

“Monkey’s gone,” I muttered.  Her shoulders slumped down at the news. I poured alak for her from a steel jug. She picked up her cup and downed half of it, slamming it against the counter with a gasp. I went on to fill my own cup, and drank it all in one swig.

“This is just another Monkey-Toto Brawl,” she announced. “He’ll come back for you.” 

"Who cares?"

Her eyes narrowed as I refilled my cup.

“What?” I asked.

She reached over the counter to run her hand through my matted hair, just like she did when I was younger. I swatted it off.

“This will blow over,” she said, her voice harsh. “He’ll be back.”

Flor knew Mama from the Pile: a floating island of garbage and scrap metal where hundreds of people lived.  They were born there and grew up together, almost like sisters, but they were separated when pirates raided the Pile, like they do every year. They took Flor along with dozens of other girls. Shortly after that, Mama left the Pile with the jeepney she rebuilt. They found each other years later when Flor approached the jeepney, drawn to the smell of the lugaw Mama and I cooked for people.

“Monkey’s gone?” Tom shrieked, his voice hoarse.  Food dribbled past his jawline, and reached his bony chest.  His body shook.  He knocked his bowl aside, and shoved his head and most of his torso into the jeepney’s side window. He looked inside, his eyes bulging as he scanned the interior, as if my huge brother would be able to hide in some dark corner.

“Did they take him too? ”

“Shut up!” I shoved him out of my jeepney.

“Calm down, Tom,” Flor yelled over the noise, pulling him back to his seat, and thumping him on the shoulder.  “Why do you hate spacers so much? They try, don’t they? I heard they’re planning on coming down earthside to fix things.”

“Bullshit,” he blubbered, snot coming down from his nose.  “All bullshit. Always the same.  They ruin everything...try to fix their mess...then ruin everything even more!”

He started to weep again, his shoulders heaving up and down.

“Oh dear. Came at a good time, did I?”

George, sat in his usual spot to my right. He was a traveling projectionist, and his family used to own a small movie theater before the islands were swallowed whole. They’ve been passing down the trade for generations, and the oversized projector rigged to his boat, as well as the reels he lugged around, have been with his family since they opened that little theater now deep underwater.

He moved through the watery wastes on a trimaran, showing movies on the side of sunken buildings or against the sails of his vessel.  People came from all parts to watch a movie. For a bowl of simple lugaw with no toppings, Monkey and I got to watch whatever movie we liked, and we’ve always pick the same thing: Enter the Dragon.  We’ve seen it about a hundred times.

“They’re whining because my brother’s gone,” I blurted out to George, trying to sound indifferent, but failing. “Apparently my presence is not enough.”

     George laughed.

     “You have been fighting with your kuya about getting off-planet for years now,” he said drily.  “You should be a good boy and listen to your older brother.”

I slammed my fist down on the counter, causing their bowls to clatter.  

“Please,” I said under my breath. “Not that stupid cliché.”

George did not flinch, and only looked at me with a raised eyebrow.  Tom laughed giddily, while Flor downed the rest of her alak.

“I belong here,” I said. “Not up there in a cold metal box, doing some undignified job like wading through shit tanks to make sure they’re working.”

“Hoy! Toto…” Tom slurred, his eyes unfocused. “What about that spacer girlfriend of yours? I heard she wants you to go up there and stay with her. She’s very pretty. Her teeth are straight and shiny.”

I had not thought about her for a few days. I made sure to avoid any streams of thought that would take me in that direction.

“We’re not talking about her.”

     Flor bit her lip, then looked up and said, “None of that matters, does it Toto? You still can’t let what happened years ago remain unresolved.”

There was silence, and only the sound of boiling water filled our ears. I poured a sack of rice into the vat.

"Toto, are you still looking for your mother after all these years?”

     “Don’t be stupid,” I mumbled.

George grinned, putting his arm over Flor awkwardly.  “Let the boy be! Toto just wants to be a man, and take care of himself out here for once without relying on Monkey.”

“And get himself killed by hopped up water rats and pirates who have a score to settle with them?” Flor screeched at George, then turned to me, and grabbed my arm. “You realize that you only have each other left? Why would you treat each other like that when so many of us—”

     “Stop, Flor” I said, shaking her off. “You're not my mother.”

Her eyes widened, and I looked away, stirring the vat. I tried not to think about the fact that Flor was the one who left food for us when Mama left years ago. She even came by every month to give us haircuts.

I ducked down as Flor, in one movement, threw a crumpled note at my face and leapt over the rubble, back into her pirogue. I grabbed it stupidly. It was a clothing allocation, the note Flor always bartered with. She somehow always had plenty of it. It was a Flor-type insult because it had been established long ago that she always ate for free with me.

George fidgeted, his mouth opening and closing, unsure of what to say. “Did you know, Toto, that in the space colonies, they do not show movies? They have these simulation rooms instead. Sounds horrid, yes?”

After the rest of them left, I watched as their vessels weaved around the jeepney then filed out of the clearing. They diverged into different directions, carving out their own respective trails on a sea of murky decay.

#

  A large caravan of vessels came through in the next few weeks.  People traveled in big groups for protection out here, but this was the largest I’ve seen yet.

“Hoy! Why are there so many of you?” I called out, while people crowded around my jeepney, pushing notes in my hands, while I slung lugaw as fast as I could.

They looked scared, and their gaze would whip back into the direction from where they came.

“We don’t know,” said an older woman. “We saw something in the horizon, the biggest, darkest cloud. Then other people were making a run for it in their boats, so we followed.”

The way she said it made me think she was a little unhinged. Everyone who survives the watery wastes into middle age eventually ends up like Tom: bitter, raw, and a little fried.

They moved at their own slow pace and lingered to talk with one another, while giving me a continuous stream of orders.   

     They lazed around for most of the evening, lounging on the rooftops of their boats, slurring stories, and sipping homebrewed alak. The water shifted and we all looked up as a white shape appeared at the outer rim of vessels.  It was a sleek, bright hydrofoil. Probably the most advanced watercraft around here. A girl climbed out of it, and jumped from one boat to another towards the jeepney.  People began to curse at her.

  “Alright!” I announced to the caravan.  “Time to clear out!”

The vessels started their engines, as she got comfortable on a piece of rubble next to the jeepney.  Everyone drifted off, leaving just the two of us.

“What do you want, Vi?” I suppressed the urge to hide my bruised face.

She smiled, showing the straight white teeth of a girl born in the space colonies.   

     “Whatever you recommend Toto.”

I took jars from the top cabinet—luxury ingredients that trickled down earthside. I made her a gingery and salty lugaw, adding a marbled tea egg, dried pork rinds, fried shallots, and dried green onion flakes.  When I put the steaming bowl on the counter, her eyes lit up.

     “You’re weird,” I said.

“Why?”

    “You could just tell your ship to make this for you,” I said, watching her stir the soup and then eating a spoonful. “You said your hydrofoil had a printer that could make anything you’d ever want to eat.”

She stared at the writings on the side of the jeepney, as if she did not hear anything, and asked: “What does it mean?”

I remembered back to when Mama explained that. In the past, jeepneys’ routes were painted on the sides so passengers would know which one to take. Mama repainted the sides of the jeepney: Ang hindi marunong lumingon sa pinanggalingan ay hindi makararating sa paroroonan.

“He who does not look back from where he came will never reach his destination,” I said.

Vi’s eyes grew wider. “It’s an old proverb?”

“Very old,” I said. “Now you have to give me one that you know. That’s how it works.”

She looked up to the sky, her gloved fingers bunched up under her chin.

“Serva me, servabo te,” she said.

“What does it mean?”

"Save me and I will save you.”

“You don’t need saving,” I said, throwing a towel over my shoulder.

“This is true,” she replied. “We have been given everything we’ll ever need.”

I rolled my eyes. In the space colonies, there was no sickness or poverty. Nanos and AI made sure that everything ran without flaws. All spacers were made in tubes, ruling out any diseases or abnormalities. The perfection was almost creepy, and I was unsettled when Vi visited the lugaw stall with a team of scientists a year ago. She was too stunning, and her smooth, expressionless face unnerved me. Back then, she moved awkwardly, unused to Earth’s gravity. Yet when I set bowls of lugaw in front of her team, Vi was the only one who ate my cooking with enthusiasm. The rest were scared of “pathogens” and never took off their surgical masks. The smell from the watery wastes was too much. Only Vi came back after that first visit.

     “The replicators never prepare it correctly, I’m afraid,” she said, cutting through my thoughts.

“Huh?”

“You asked if our replicators could make lugaw,” she explained, one eyebrow raised. “They can, but it will never be as lovely as how you prepare it.”

I looked away, trying to hide my face, which was somehow getting hotter by the minute. I was not sure if she was teasing me, especially since she was still trying to understand sarcasm. I laughed, realizing that she was nowhere near that spacer I first met.

She paused, and looked around.  “It looks like your brother has gone. Has he found the means to leave the planet?”

I didn’t say anything, so she pressed on. “I do wish you would change your mind and go with your brother.”

    “Why do people keep pestering me about this?” I wondered out loud, annoyed.  “I told you my answer the last time you were here.”

   She put her spoon down, urgency suddenly lining her face.

“Because you might not survive if you stay,” she said, her voice a tenor I had not heard before. It was fear. “My team is recreating the storms.”

I felt a headache coming on, intensified by alak given to me by my customers who were eager to set me in a good mood. I suddenly remembered the old woman who warned me about the giant black cloud on the horizon.

    “Why are you telling me this?” I asked. “Why do you care?”

    “Of course I care,” she said. “I thought you would find it beautiful. We’ve discovered that the same series of supercyclones that destroyed this archipelago can also revitalize it.”

     I flung the ladle against the wall, and the clattering sound reverberated throughout the jeepney.  

       “Why don’t you spacers just leave us alone?” I said, rubbing my forehead.  “Tom was right.  You say you’re going to fix things, but you’re just making everything worse!”

    “This has always been my mission,” she said, defensively.  “The storms will clean up all the debris and waste that has been stagnating in these parts for hundreds of years, and turn them into new atolls. Everything will be new. There will be more land for crops, lumber, and livestock for the space colonies. And we’ll be able to fill our generation ships in a few years!”

According to the spacers, the generation ships were going to be the salvation of humanity. Several were being built to go across the stars to colonize new worlds, although I couldn’t care less. They were going to revitalize the watery wastes, not for the people that still lived here, but for their own goals.

     “And do you also plan to wash us away in the process?”  

“No!” she said, quickly. “I’m here to ask again if you would come with me. I’ve already planned for you to—”

     The sound of shrieking voices cut through our conversation, making my skin crawl. I looked out the window to find two large, dilapidated fishing boats coming towards the jeepney from either direction, with dozens of kids standing on both decks. I half-ran towards my blaster, but instead went back, and pulled Vi into the jeepney before a rain of shots hit the vehicle from both sides.

     Four water rats scrambled in through the back, grabbing for anything they thought they could sell or sniff.  Most of them were around my age. They had a sunken, glazed look.  Their bones protruded from their shoulders and knees, and I could see the red veiny threads in their eyes.  They were all coming down bad, itching for a sniff of chupa.   A small kid, who only looked to be ten, grabbed my blaster and pointed it at us. The only one who looked sober swung himself next to Vi and pressed the steel against her neck.

     It was Pock.  

     Monkey and I knew him from the floating rings. He once lost a huge bet because of my brother.

      “Be cool, Pock,” I said evenly, even though my hands shook. I pointed at the counter. “Notes are in that jar.”

     Everyone knew better than to fight water rats high on chupa.  Once they took a hit, they moved faster than normal and could snap off your neck in one go.  Monkey told me that he once saw a little kid, around five or six, bite someone’s cheek off.

     Pock let go of Vi and stuffed the notes into his pockets. She sank down next to me on the floorboards, deathly quiet. Pock laughed giddily.  He caught me rolling my eyes, then smirked.

“Did that giant finally get tired of watching out for your sorry ass?”

“Get out of my jeepney,” I said.  He snorted and jumped back to the rails of their fishing boat.  I let out a breath of relief, as we watched them file out the back entrance.  They usually came in and out in a flash, leaving with notes, but also random things that were not necessarily of value to most people.

     “We’re taking the two of them with us,” he announced, to a sea of moans from his crew.

“We don’t like them,” someone whined.

“No complaining! ” he said sharply.

My heart suddenly wanted to burst out of my chest.

“There is no way in hell—” I spat.  

Someone punched me in the jaw.  Vi stayed still and watched me fall to the floor, her breath coming in quickly.

     I pushed my tongue against a molar that suddenly came loose.  “Let my friend go, or you’ll piss off her trigger-happy spacer friends.”

     “I think I’ll take that risk,” Pock laughed, eyeing Vi, as two members of his crew dragged us out of the jeepney. I saw her nostrils flare, staring angrily at Pock, before they blindfolded me.

#

     It took almost a day before we slowed down to a near stop. They took off my blindfold, as the twin fishing boats floated past a graveyard of scrapped ship hulls that have been left to rot.  The water rats’ turf was in a crumbling cargo ship.   As we approached, their ship grew larger and larger until it towered over our heads.  It carried hollow metal boxes stacked high above the watery wastes.   Each kid seemed to have his own trailer. After dragging me onboard, I watched as one of them scaled up to the highest one, twelve trailers high.  He peeked out from the opening, sniffing yellow stuff through a plastic bag.

  Another kid shoved a little bag onto my hand when I stepped onto the deck.  I told them to go to hell, to which they responded by holding me down, and I either had to breathe into the plastic bag or not at all.  It smelled like half piss and half gasoline.  It made me dizzy, but I didn’t get high.  I just retched all over their grimy deck while they laughed. I knew better the next turn.  I pretended to inhale and get into it.  They left me alone after that, and threw me into the ship’s moldy kitchen.  When I saw that they were taking Vi somewhere else, I tried to run and reach out to her, but they shoved me back.

“Vi!”

She looked over her shoulder as a group of them gathered around me, pushed me down, and began kicking me repeatedly.

“Make us something to eat,” they demanded afterwards.

#

Monkey said once that chupa was the easiest way to forget about hunger. The water rats seemed to prefer chupa to coming into the kitchen and trying to cook.  It was a slimy place, and parts of the walls were blackened from fires over the years.

  Once I was able to stand, I walked into the back room and found a pantry where the only light came from multiple blaster holes in the ceiling. They reminded me of stars in the night sky.  Sacks of rice were stacked high, almost reaching the ceiling. The symbol of the Amalgamated Space Colonies was emblazoned on each sack: eighteen stars encircling a sphere. In another corner, there was a row of chocolate cake mix staring back at me. Airdrop rations from the colonies for the people left behind.

I made a garlicky lugaw so pungent that the whole ship filled with the smell.  The crew slid down from the towers of trailers, as I brought the vat into the main deck.

“There was all this food down there,” I told Pock.  “Why didn’t anyone make anything out of it?”

“Chupa’s easier,” he shrugged.

I scooped lugaw into old cans.

“Hurry up, they’re hungry!” Pock snarled, grabbing the cans from me and distributing them to his crew. I scanned the deck, as I doled out the lugaw quickly, my mind plotting out the best routes for escape. The trailers formed a maze on the deck, and I mapped out the fastest path to the fishing boats that were docked around the cargo ship. Vi and I would have to sprint through the maze, and jump onto the boat that would get us out of Pock’s turf the fastest.

They all ate in silence, although there was the occasional loud slurping noise. As the evening wore on, the glazed looks on their faces were replaced with something else.  A bit of color came to their cheeks after being able to eat something warm. When I asked if they could send a bowl to Vi, they complied.  When I asked to see her, they ignored me.

     “Will you at least tell me why you’re holding us here?” I asked.  “To get back at my brother? He’s long gone, you know.”

     Pock gestured towards me and his second-in-command rammed his fist into my stomach. I bent over with a grunt.

“You talk too much.”

     I looked up and glared at Pock.

     “You’re a real pain in the ass aren’t you,” he laughed, slapping his thighs.  He was sitting on top of a trailer. “Do you have something else stupid to say?”

     “How did you get so many sacks of rice?”

     He shrugged.

     “Eventually, they’ll forget about the people they left behind,” he said after a while with a sobering look, “and those rations will stop coming.”

     “What the hell are you talking about?” I asked.  “Are you trying to go to the space colonies too?”

“I dare you ask more stupid questions,” he spat.

     “My cousin was able to work up there,” a boy piped in. “He got assigned to be a feeder in a generation ship.”

     “I heard he gets paired up with dozens of different girls,” Pock’s second added.  “Sounds like the life to me!”

I turned around to find the second thrusting his hips back and forth.  They all broke out in laughter, but I stayed quiet. Advancing humanity or no, the idea of making babies just to keep a ship going sounded miserable.

“When the ship finally makes it across the stars to a new planet,” the boy said, frowning, “my cousin will be long dead.”

“His great-great-great-grandchildren will be the only ones to see it,” Pock said.  “If they even make it.”

“I heard when someone dies,” the second added, “they burn the body and just sprinkle what’s left back into the crops inside the ships.”

“All the while the spacers get to sleep in ice until the ship gets there,” Pock said bitterly.  “And when your cousin’s great-great-grand-babies get there, they still have to work for the spacers.”

“Here, we live for ourselves!” the second yelled.

They all cheered and banged their bowls against the rusty deck as I brought out dessert.

“What is that?”

“It’s chocolate cake,” I said.

Pock scooped a piece out of the pan with his hand, crumbs falling all over the deck.  He took a bite and grew quiet.  Something entered his expression.  He closed his eyes and started chewing slowly, savoring each bite. The crew converged towards me in a rush of noise and fighting.  Someone fell to the ground with a cut lip, just as a flurry of hands dove into the pan.  It was gone in seconds. After dinner, they rubbed their distended bellies with wonder, unused to the feeling of fullness. They crawled back to their trailers, their snores echoing against the hollow metal walls.

#

After that night, they locked me in the kitchen for three days.  They did not let me out once, not even to serve them food, no matter how much I yelled.  I eventually had to find a way out. I picked at the old, rotting lock with a metal skewer I found in a drawer.  When I managed to break through, it was still a few hours before sunrise.  The whole ship was pitch black. I checked every level for Vi, but had no luck.  I found a blaster leaning against the wall and took it. When I reached the main deck, I froze at the sound of conversation and a gentle whirring.

     “You only have nineteen children here.” It was an older male voice, with the lilt of someone from the space colonies.  “You said you would have twenty.”

     “Don’t get your undies in a twist,” Pock said.  “One of my boys tweaked too hard and died, so I picked up an extra body on my way back.”

“I still cannot understand why you let them use inhalants.”

“I do my job, you do yours,” Pock said. “You’ve been happy with my system so far, haven’t you? I take in these boys to help me gather warm bodies for your ships, and when they’ve done their time, I send them off to you too. If I save notes here and there letting them sniff instead of eat, all the better.”

    I peeked from behind a trailer and saw a compact air shuttle idling on the deck.  There were dozens of spacers standing in line, wearing white uniforms with the Amalgamated Space Colonies logo emblazoned on their back.  An unconscious kid was in each person’s arms, as they marched into the shuttle.  But before they went through the bay doors, someone in a lab jacket injected a blue liquid at the base of each kid’s skull.  Pock stood next to a man in white.  Vi stood on the man’s other side.

     I walked towards them, blaster pointed at Pock.

     “The hell?” I screamed. “What’s happening to your crew?”

     At the sight of me, the people shuffled behind the bay doors, while Vi and the man who looked to be her superior faced me.

    Pock only laughed.  

     “Don’t look at me,” he said.  “They’re the ones that want the bodies to fill those ships.”

     Vi stepped forward and held her hands up.

     “Toto,” she said, her bright eyes locking with mine.  “Put that down.”

     “No!” I screamed again, aiming the blaster at her. “Let them go!”

     The man next to her drew a small silver cylinder with the flick of his wrist, and a bolt of light shot out of it.  I froze in place. Someone ran out from the hatch and tackled me to the floor, while another yanked the blaster from my grip.  I had lost all feeling to my limbs.

     “See, now? This is why reconditioning is vital when it comes to these savages,” the man muttered to Vi, as if he was just continuing an earlier conversation. “Like rabid dogs, honestly.”

“Yes, sir.”

“Hoy!” Pock yelled out, interrupting their conversation. One of the spacers just dropped a boy. “Careful!”

There was a pause.

“Are you serious?” asked the man. “You were the one that laced their food.”

I felt sick at hearing this. Pock had insisted that he be the one to serve the lugaw to his crew a few days ago. The only reason I was conscious was because I served myself.

“Look, I know they won’t remember their life here, either way,” Pock said. “But I don’t want them getting knocked around like that!”

“Vi,” said the man, kneeling over me. “Let’s please not keep Pock waiting and disperse the adequate funds to him, while I introduce the reconditioning serum for this one here.”

Pock laughed. “You know me too well.”

“Of course. You have been my best body gatherer these past few years,” the man said.

“I’m just doing a service to these people. This place is filthy and depressing,” Pock rattled on. “These boys are lucky they’ll get out before the storms come.”

“Vi,” I called out to her, pleading.

“Don’t be silly, darling,” the man said under his breath. “You have been acting very odd lately. Do remind me to schedule you for psychiatric evaluation when we return.”

    He pushed her aside, and the man rolled me to my back.  From the corner of my eye, I saw him pull out a syringe with the same blue liquid used on the other kids.  I felt a pinprick, but it stopped.  Suddenly, there was the sound of boot colliding against flesh, then bones cracking.  It was followed by multiple shots firing in the air.  I saw a flurry of legs running in every direction.  I felt something pull me up, and my head lolled to my side to find Vi, blood all over her white suit, holding the blaster.  My muscles were like jelly, and almost all my weight was on her.  Her father was screaming, both hands gripping his bloody leg, while two other spacers were on the ground nearby, wounds blooming on their arm and shoulder. Pock was rocking back and forth on the floor, his hand bloody.

“I was doing those water rats a favor!” he screamed to me. “There’s nothing else for them here!”

Vi dragged me through the maze of trailers. We stood briefly at the edge of the deck, before jumping onto the roof of a hydrofoil.  It was a larger model than the one she operated. I was reeling with questions, but the blood on her was the only thing I could see.

“Vi,” my tongue felt heavy. I went in and out of consciousness. “A-Are you alright?”

She deposited me on the floor of the cabin.  I heard the gentle whirring of the hydrofoil as she took control of it and we sped off over the water.

“Not to worry,” she said. “It’s not my blood.”

The hydrofoil began to rock violently. The floor came up to meet me as the vessel tipped up, only to be dropped back again.

“What’s happening?”

I must have dozed off. I looked through the windows to find the clouds swirling gray and black. Lightning flashed in several directions all around us, and the sound of the wind was deafening. I couldn’t hold it in anymore and I puked on the pristine white floor. 

She looked back at me with worry crinkling in her eyes, her palms still pressed firmly on the two pulsating panels that were used to navigate the hydrofoil.  I’ve never been inside of a watercraft built by spacers. I could only admire the sleek interior for a few seconds before I puked again.  Panels lit up in urgency all around me, making my vision spin.

“The storm has started!” she yelled over the howling winds. There was a gleam of excitement in her eye.

“I feel like someone turned me inside out.”

“Your body is rejecting the reconditioning nanos,” she said, almost sounding impressed.  She let go of the controls and the hydrofoil began to navigate itself. She came to me and knelt down, placing a blanket over my shoulders.  

“For certain people, it takes several rounds for their body to adapt.”

“Reconditioning?”

“Yes,” she said.  “It’s the process of turning a person into a shell with no memory or willpower. The perfect worker for long-term space travel.”

     “Oh.” I didn’t think I could be any more nauseous.  “So you mess with our heads, because you think we’re too stupid to think for ourselves? Because we weren’t designed to be perfect like you spacers?”

     “Even we are implanted with nanos,” she said. “Except we are at least granted some degree of control.  Those orphans who were part of Pock’s crew will likely have no autonomy.  They will simply be auxiliaries—physical extensions of whatever generation ship they will be assigned to.”

     “That’s disgusting,” I threw the blanket to the side.  “What about the people who sneak into the space colonies? What about my brother? Will they shove that thing into the back of his skull too?”

“No,” she said uncertainly, her face in her hands. “Monkey will blend in with the population and find work. He must. ”

She then crumpled to the floor.

    “Vi!” I pulled her up, and held her awkwardly. She was much heavier than she looked. I caught myself feeling self-conscious about my breath, and suddenly felt stupid.

    “I’m sorry,” she said, smiling.  “I’m just exhausted. I’ve never done anything like that before.”

“Done what?”

“Disobeyed.”

    “Why did you bother?” I asked. I wondered if she would ever be able to go back after what she has done.

Terror filled her eyes. “I do not wish reconditioning on my worst enemy. It is an abhorrent practice that they use on only the worst criminals. When I found out that they were stealing people away all these years—”

“How could you have not known this?” I left her leaning against the wall, and stood, stumbling to the other side of the wildly swaying hydrofoil, away from her. “There…have been people that have gone missing in the watery wastes. What if it was all because of crazy spacers obsessed with firing people into the stars?”

An image of my mother flickered into my mind, her mouth forming the promise that she would be back. It had been years, and I no longer remembered the sound of her voice.

I turned back to Vi and she regarded me solemnly, her lower lip quivering. “I am so sorry, Toto. Working and living under my father, I had my suspicions, but it seemed like such a ridiculous idea. Then when that man decided to take you right in front of me, and my father appeared…I understand if you do not trust me.”

I shook my head. “How could your people allow this?”

“Anything to advance humanity,” she said. “That is what we have been taught.”

She flinched, gripping her side. I went to her and pulled her hand to mine. Blood dripped through her fingers.

“You lied,” I told her. “They shot you.”

She ignored me for a long moment, and closed her eyes.

     “I think I understand now why you insisted on staying in the watery wastes,” she said, her voice even. “It is dangerous here.  There is no order. It is utter chaos--but it is yours.”

“Then why did you try to get me to leave everything behind just a few days ago?”

“Because you are an amazing cook,” she said, without missing a beat, although her eyes were crinkling

I laughed at that. After a year, she finally knew how to tell a joke.

“But it wouldn’t taste the same up there,” I said.

“Precisely.”

After a moment, I shared the blanket with her.

“Listen, Vi,” I said. “Let’s make a deal. If we survive this storm, you have to help me. I don’t want anymore of my people getting taken against their will and turned into mindless drones. If you agree, I will consider you my friend.”

It was her turn to laugh. “That’s it?”

“Friends always eat for free.”

She smiled, her eyes excited. “I will help you.”

“And after we hunt down the body gatherers, you have to take me to the space colonies.”

There was a sharp intake of breath under the blanket.

“After all this time? Are you sure?”

“Trust me, I really don’t want to go to such a boring-sounding place,” I said, grinning. “But I have no choice. I have to make sure Monkey is okay.”

We shivered as we watched the storm pull the rubble, waste, and debris down into the depths of the sea. Vi whispered as we huddled under the blanket that all that junk would be gathered and formed into atolls in the next few days. We held onto the rails to stop from rolling back and forth across the deck.

In the distance, something flickered between the waves. The glimmer of the jeepney’s chrome plating flashed bright as lightning lit up the sky. Despite the never-ending waves that threatened to drive it underneath, it fought to stay afloat.
